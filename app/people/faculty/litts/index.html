Bio

Dr. Breanne Litts is an explorer at heart. When she’s not working, you might find her out hiking, rock climbing, and enjoying the outdoors.

In her research, Breanne investigates how people learn and how we can use technology to bring people together in new, productive ways. Breanne started the LED Lab to investigate how people learn through making, designing, and producing.

Breanne’s students love the sense of community they find through the LED Lab. Breanne supports her students in their own projects and encourages them to take part in her research. She organizes fun outings for her students, like rock climbing and axe throwing.

Breanne serves on the Evaluation and Assessment team and the Advisory Board for the Cultural Competence Grant. Learn more about Breanne here: https://circlcenter.org/meet-breanne-litts 

Breanne is looking for a diverse group of students to join in her research. She values the way people from multiple disciplines and backgrounds work together. Her approach to research is partnership, equity, and design oriented.

Featured Work

Learn Explore Design Lab

The LED Lab investigates how people learn and collaborate through making, designing, and producing. Students are welcome to join the lab and develop projects around their own interests or join in on current projects. See more at https://learnexploredesign.org/

The Boi Ogoi Cultural Interpretive Center

The Boa Ogoi Cultural Interpretive Center in Salt Lake seeks to educate and enlighten visitors about the history of the Northwestern Shoshone Band. Breanne Litts and her team work alongside the organization to collect and document stories around the Bear River Massacre site, providing design and tech support. Learn more at https://boaogoi.org/.

EAGER Grant: Culturally Responsive Making

The goal of the Culturally Responsive Making project is to develop culturally responsive making activities with two indigenous communities in Utah and Arizona, and to investigate how these spaces and activities impact youth, teacher, and community conceptions of STEM learning.  Learn more about the project here and about the NSF award here.

Learning in the Making: A Comparative Case Study of Three Makerspaces

Through a comparative, in-depth case study of three makerspaces, the authors explore how makerspaces function as learning environments how participants learn and develop through complex design and making practices. Learn more here.

GetMobile: Mobile Computing and Communications

The proliferation of smartphones provides easy access to augmented reality experiences. In this column, the authors present an approach by which we can provide all young people with equal access to AR, and how we can empower students to be producers, not just consumers. Learn more here.

Research Focus

Breanne Litts investigates how people learn and collaborate through making, designing, and producing. She develops technologies and learning environments to support these interdisciplinary activities. Breanne examines how to leverage place-based storytelling to engage young people. Her interest extends to how place-based storytelling engages youth in design practices for both cultural and civic issues, as well as computational and technological.

Breanne’s overarching goal is to democratize disciplinary learning and legitimize making as learning. She collaborates and co-designs with academic, K-12, library, museum, industry, and other community partners. With NSF support (AISL #1623404), Dr. Litts currently works with her co-PI’s and in partnership with American Indian communities to co-design culturally responsive maker activities and spaces and support diverse knowledge systems in disciplinary-based learning environments by leveraging narrative- and place-based approaches.

Her scholarly interests combine identity, learning, design, and technology, particularly from a learning sciences perspective.

  • How we construct our identities through story and place
  • How people learn through designing/making, especially at the intersection of digital and physical worlds
  • How people collaborate across disciplines, communities, and cultures
