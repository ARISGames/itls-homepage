Bio

Professor Victor Lee is interested in using new technologies to support the teaching and learning of STEM content and practices in both formal and informal settings and across age groups. His professional work has involved embodied learning, wearable technologies, and Maker education.

Victor’s classes are casual in format. He encourages his students to be balanced. He supports their personal lives and needs.

Victor was a graduate fellow with the NSF-funded Center for Curriculum Materials in Science, past recipient of the American Educational Research Association's SIG ATL/EST - Best Student Paper award, an NSF CAREER award, the Jan Hawkins Early Career award, and a National Academy of Education/Spencer Foundation Postdoctoral Fellowship. He served as Chair of AERA's Special Interest Group for Advanced Technologies for Learning and is on the board of directors for the International Society of the Learning Sciences.

In addition to his research, Victor co-advises the USU Esports Club. This new club exists to provide a home for hobbyists and competitive esports players. Victor is currently doing some research on esports teams’ use of data. He helped develop the esports studio at USU, which has six high-quality gaming machines.

Victor is looking for students who like to work independently and who are interested in issues of STEM education. He is also looking for students who are interested in or having experience with analyzing video.

Featured Work

Learning Technologies and the Body (published 2015)

This collection of writings explores the most current uses of body-responsive technologies, and how these technologies are being used to advance the future of educational technology. Contributors explore how technology-supported learning environments can incorporate physical activity and interactive experiences in formal education.

Reconceptualizing Libraries

Reconceptualizing Libraries: Perspectives from the Information and Learning Sciences, 2018, with Abigail Phillips  identifies the potential for libraries to adapt and transform in the wake of new technologies for connected learning and discovery. The volume brings together cases and models developed by ITLS experts to examine the new kinds of education research taking place in libraries.

The PAD Project

The goal of this project is to understand how Physical Activity Data (PAD) devices, like wearables and activity trackers, can be used as a data source for personal data analysis. The project implemented PAD devices in two fifth grade classrooms and looked at how kids used and learned from these interventions. The project also produced a curriculum to teach data and statistics content.  Find out more at: https://digitalcommons.usu.edu/itls_facpub/252/

Tabletop to Screen

The goal of this project is to look at computer science-themed board games as an unplugged approach to introducing computer science concepts. Students will learn through tabletop games and then reproduce what they learn in a programming environment to formalize their understanding into code. This project will produce a unit of curriculum. (2019-2021 Research Grant through NSF)

Research Focus

Victor’s current work involves designing supports for libraries to incorporate maker programs for youth patrons and using new technologies to detect youth engagement in afterschool maker programs. Topics include visual representations, curriculum design, cognitive science, everyday and intuitive reasoning, STEM conceptual change, wearable technology, personal informatics, and the Maker Movement.

Other current projects include studying unplugged and screen free computer science education for elementary students. Victor has two current grants from NSF studying this topic.

Past work included using fitness trackers as tools for students to learn data science, studying the Quantified Self movement and its implications for education, researching intuitive and embodied reasoning in clinical interviews, and more recently, health education (related to diabetes and self tracking of data.)

Victor’s research is currently exploring the following areas:

  • Design supports for libraries to incorporate maker programs.
  • Studying unplugged and screen free computer science education
  • Studying the quantified self movement
  • Intuitive and embodied reasoning in Physics and health education
